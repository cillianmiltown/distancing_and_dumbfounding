Temporal Distancing and Dumbfounding

Overview of Judgments

Three thousand Four hundred Eight participants (71%) rated the behavior of Julie and Mark as wrong initially, and three thousand two hundred Seventeen participants (67.02%) rated the behavior as wrong at the end of the task. There was a significant difference between initial ratings (M = 3.3, SD = 1.3) and revised ratings (M = 3.4, SD = 1.4), t(4799) = -4.67, p < .001, d = 0.07.

Distancing and Judgments Made

There was no difference in initial judgement depending on distance manipulation: F(2, 4797) = 0.41, p = .665 \(\eta\)p2 = 0, (Mincreased = 3.3, SDincreased = 1.3, Mdecreased = 3.2, SDdecreased = 1.3, Mcontrol = 3.3, SDcontrol = 1.3). There was no difference in revised judgement depending on distance manipulation: F(2, 4797) = 0.49, p = .616, \(\eta\)p2 = 0, (Mincreased = 3.4, SDincreased = 1.4, Mdecreased = 3.4, SDdecreased = 1.4, Mcontrol = 3.4, SDcontrol = 1.4).

Distancing and Reason-Giving/Dumbfounding

There was a significant association between temporal distance condition and response to the critical slide, \(\chi\)2(4, N = 4800) = 108.068, p < .001, V = 0.15, the observed power was 1. The responses to the critical slide for the increased distance group (N = 1600) the decreased distance group (N = 1600), and the control group (N = 1600) are displayed in {apafg-tempoverall}.
